About Me

I'm a current doctoral student at the University of Oregon. I enjoy using data analysis
as a tool to understand the world. I have been teaching statistics and data analysis
for seven years.

I'm also a psychology researcher interested in many different topics, including norms,
unsustainable consumption, and shared reality.

Currently, I'm working on a project investigating how norm interventions can be used
to reduce peoples' unsustainable consumption, which is the consumption of goods and
services with negative environmental impacts at levels not sustainable long-term
by the planet.

I'm also very interested in the critique of current scientific practices and how we,
as a scientific community, can make improvements to strengthen our field's body of
literature. This includes joining the open science movement, normalizing
pre-registration, and updating the norms around publication practices.


Education:

-- M.A. in Social Psychology, San Francisco State University, 2016

Presentations & Publications
Perspective taking: Motivation and impediment to shared reality

This paper was an exploration of the relationship between the concept of shared reality and perspective taking.

Where perspective taking can and cannot take us

This paper elaborated on the benefits and limitations of perspective taking as a tool used in interpersonal relationships.

Weak emission-line quasars in the context of a modified baldwin effect

As an undergraduate, I studied math, chemistry, and astronomy. I completed an independent study project investigating the qualities of quasars, which are high-energy objects that existed in the early universe.

Unpopular Norms among College Students

There is a peculiar phenomenon identified in sociology called 'unpopular norms', which are norms that guide the everyday behavior of members of a group that secretly the group members actually disagree with. In this project, I investigated unpopular norms that exist among college students.

The role of personality in predicting display rules

Display rules are informal rules that exist among cultural groups regarding how acceptable it is for individuals to express different types of emotions. In this study, I investigated the relative importance of individual-level traits, specifically personality, and cultural-level factors, display rules, for predicting peoples' expression of different emotions.

The relationship between display rules in relational contexts and collectivistic values

This study investigated whether differences in how one expresses one emotions to close others, such as one's parents, versus distant others, such as strangers, could be explained by how much individuals value collectivism, which is a value emphasizing the importance of relational harmony.
